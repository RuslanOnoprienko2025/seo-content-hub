## What to Include in Your Student Email Signature

Your signature answers the questions recipients ask immediately: What's your academic level? What field? Which institution? How do I respond?

### Essential Elements for Students

Build your email signature with these components:

**Full Name:** Use the name that matches your academic records and any published work. Consistency matters for professional recognition.

**Academic Status and Program:** State your exact level and field—"Undergraduate Student in Economics," "Graduate Student in Computer Science," "PhD Candidate in Molecular Biology." The distinction between "graduate student," "PhD student," and "PhD candidate" communicates where you are in your academic journey and what level of expertise recipients can expect.

**University and Department:** Small institutions need just the university name. At large research universities, add your department—"MIT" tells recipients almost nothing, while "Department of Biology, MIT" places you in a specific scholarly community where collaboration happens.

**University Email Address:** Your institutional .edu address proves you're affiliated with a legitimate academic institution. Personal email addresses from consumer services raise questions about whether you're actually a student at the institution you claim.

**Phone Number:** Include your direct line when coordination requires real-time contact—scheduling lab meetings, arranging conference calls, handling time-critical research logistics.

**Personal Website or LinkedIn:** This is where you maintain your academic presence—publication lists, conference presentations, research interests, downloadable CV.

## The Text-Only HTML Signature

Many universities mandate official email signature templates that include university logos and strict brand compliance. While these policies aim to maintain institutional consistency, they create practical problems for students:

**Image Dependency:** Required university logos are image files that email clients block by default. Recipients see empty placeholders instead of professional branding—defeating the policy's purpose.

**Limited Flexibility:** Standardized templates often provide only generic titles like "Student" or "Graduate Student," lacking space for program specifics, research focus, or academic specializations that matter in scholarly communication.

**Technical Complexity:** Official generator tools add bureaucratic steps and approval processes to what should be a simple task. Students face multi-week delays for basic signature updates when changing programs or adding research positions.

**The Practical Alternative:** Clean, text-based HTML signatures accomplish what university policies intend—professional, consistent communication—without technical dependencies or bureaucratic barriers.

Text-only HTML isn't the plain text your email client defaults to. It's clean code that creates clickable links and proper spacing without images, complex tables, or elaborate styling.

Think of it as HTML stripped down to essentials: structured information that works identically whether your recipient opens email on Outlook for Windows, Gmail on Chrome, Apple Mail on iPhone, or Thunderbird on Linux.

The HTML provides functionality—click a phone number to call, click an email to compose, click a URL to visit. The "text-only" means no visual elements that break, block, or render inconsistently.

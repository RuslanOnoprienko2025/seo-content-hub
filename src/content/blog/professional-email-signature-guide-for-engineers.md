## What to Include in an Email Signature

Every email signature needs six essential elements. Together, they establish identity, qualifications, and accessibility in a single, scannable block. The engineer's signature credentials—particularly P.E. or EIT designations—should be correctly formatted alongside your name, title, and contact information.

### Essential 6 Fields:

**1. Full Name with Credentials**

Include your name exactly as it appears on your P.E. license, followed by your credentials. Examples: "John Smith, P.E." / "Sarah Davis, PE, LEED AP" / "Michael Chen, P.E., S.E."

Credential formatting matters. Some states use "P.E." with periods, others use "PE" without. Match your state's official format. Additional credentials (S.E., LEED AP, EIT) follow the P.E. designation, separated by commas.

**2. Professional Title**

State your discipline and level clearly. Examples: "Mechanical Engineer", "Electrical Engineer". Specific titles communicate expertise areas immediately. Generic "Engineer" titles require recipients to guess your specialization.

**3. Company**

Your firm name or employer establishes organizational context. "Martinez & Associates", "Power Systems Inc."—company names tell clients and project managers whether you're with a consulting firm, manufacturer, or contractor.

This information helps recipients understand your role. A consultant at a small firm handles different responsibilities than a professional at a large corporation. Company context matters for project coordination and technical authority.

**4. Phone Number**

Include your direct line. Clients may call about project specifications, emergency coordination during construction, or urgent technical questions from contractors. Time-sensitive work requires immediate phone access—email alone can't meet these needs.

**5. Email Address**

Professional email using your company domain. Your email address appears in recipients' contact records and reply-to fields. Company domain email signals legitimate firm affiliation—critical when clients and regulatory agencies verify credentials.

**6. Website**

Your company website or professional profile provides additional verification. Clients research firm capabilities. Project managers check company background. Regulatory agencies confirm business registration. One clear link to your primary professional web presence.
